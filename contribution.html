Inventions whilst at Fairchild Semiconductor

Whilst at Fairchild, Moore established himself as one of Fairchild's principal technologists and managers.
First, he developed the firms diffusion processes and built it diffusion furnaces, after this he worked on manufacturing processes for creating the transistor's contacts, and he eventually settled on aluminium.
Under his watch, the planar process was also created, which provided a smooth silicon dioxide surface on which would be printed the circuits of the first integrated circuit.
Under Moore's leadership, Fairchild also made contributions to the development of the metal oxide semiconductor field-effect transistor (MOSFET), the most commonly used type of transistor used in today's chips.

Moore's Law

In an interview for a special edition of the journal "Electronics", Moore was asked to make predictions about developments that would occur over the next decade. Having reviewed past data for increases in the number of transistors per silicon chip, Moore theorised that the number of transistors per silicon chip would double each year. This became known as "Moore's Law". In 1975, he revised this prediction so that the number of transistors per silicon chip would double every two years. This was seen as pessimistic at the time, as the current rate was a doubling of transistors per silicon chip every eighteen months.

Intel

When Moore worked for Fairchild, he realised that no matter how much scientific research went into the conception of silicon wafers, there would always be an artlike skill associated with their production. When he left Fairchild, both Moore and Robert Noyce decided to merge theory and practice by forcing research scientists to work directly on chips, especially the MOSFET memory chips that became Intel's first major success. Intel was also the first company to make commercially available MOSFET chips.
In 1971, Intel created the world's first commercially available microprocessor, which represented a notable advance in the technology of the integrated circuit, as it miniaturised the central processing unit of a computer, allowing smaller machines to perform calculations originally only large machines could perform.
